College Essay

Upon entering high school, one thing I became excited about was an opportunity to join the school’s robotics club. Ever since learning of the club’s existence, I was eager to participate in their projects. I had imagined building innovative robots and being successful in competition. When the first day of school came to an end, I navigated the school’s tortuous hallways to a hidden corridor where I entered through a flimsy wooden door. I was greeted with a warm welcome, given an overview of the club, and then shadowed a senior member to gain familiarity with the robotic parts and code for programming. Everything seemed well until the annual First Tech Challenge (FTC) competition video was released with a demanding task. As excited as I was to work with robots and computers, I soon realized the tall order before us.

An argument would ensue virtually every meeting on whose ideas were the best and how they should be applied to the robot. We could never come to a consensus on a design for it. Each meeting would result in a complete redesign of the robot without drawing out plans or discussing the failure of the previous design. It was chaos. The FTC robotics qualifier soon arrived, and the same disarray in the classroom carried over to the playing field. The constant redesign and rebuilding resulted in an incomplete robot at the time of competition. Parts were inoperable, and the lack of time led to careless wiring and a battery blowout. Our struggles during competition were disappointing. It was especially painful for me because I was both passionate about robotics and deeply invested in the club. What made things more demoralizing was that by junior year only three of us remained. A part of me felt like quitting, but my desire for the club to succeed motivated me to continue. This led me to reassess the future of the club and what needed to be done to attain success.

What I had learned thus far was that we needed good leadership and a better way of doing things. I felt a greater responsibility to take the lead. The first task was to publicize the club so we could recruit additional talent that could help the club. Having acquired new members, we assigned each person a specific role. I then took initiative to resolve old problems and prevent them from returning. Doing away with poor habits was a start; however, more changes needed to be made. Simple adjustments, including organizing our equipment and partitioning responsibility for coding and building, made the club a more fun and efficient environment. I created a standard by which we would focus on one idea and not deviate from it until it was thoroughly tested and assessed.

After revamping the club and introducing new collaborative ideas, our robot made it to the regional competition and claimed third place. Along the way I learned the importance of having ambition, strong leadership, and effective methodology. Individual ambition has to be balanced with the team’s goals in order to be successful. We started with three members, yet we did not give up and we went out to recruit and teach people. Leadership is not just about throwing out commands. Becoming a good listener allowed our team to consider different opinions which would be elaborated on. We avoided arguing, instead we had discussions to improve ideas. Partitioning the responsibilities of the club based on people’s strengths allowed for a more efficient team.

Nothing came easy. Our club persevered and learned to work with what we had. This experience has allowed me to face demanding challenges that presented themselves not only in the engineering process, but also while working as a team towards a common goal. The technical and software knowledge from the FTC has enhanced my abilities to face more advanced challenges in the future.
